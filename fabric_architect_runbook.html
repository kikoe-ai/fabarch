You cannot architect what you do not understand. Before opening Fabric, before drawing a diagram, before writing a single line — you need to understand the project, the team, the data, the politics, and the deadline. This phase is entirely conversations and notes.
Project and Delivery Questions
Project context
What is the project deadline and is it fixed or flexible?
Determines how aggressive the build plan needs to be
What does success look like at the end of the engagement?
Aligns your definition of done with the client's
Is there a project manager or are you also managing delivery?
Clarifies whether you own the plan or just the architecture
What delivery methodology does the client use — Agile, Waterfall, or hybrid?
Determines how you structure the build phase
Are there any regulatory or compliance requirements?
GDPR, HIPAA, SOC2 all affect architecture decisions early
What is the budget for Azure infrastructure?
Directly determines which capacity SKU is realistic
Team structure
Who is on the data team — names, roles, and experience levels?
You need to know who can do what before assigning work
What is the team's experience with Microsoft Fabric and Azure?
Determines how much hand-holding vs independence you plan for
Are team members permanent employees or contractors?
Contractors may leave — permanent staff need more knowledge transfer
Who will own the platform after you leave?
That person needs to be shadowing you from Week 1, not Week 20
Who makes technical decisions — is it you alone or committee?
Committees slow everything down — understand the decision chain early
Is there an existing DBA, data engineer, or BI developer on the team?
Existing skills shape how you divide work across the team
Stakeholders
Who is the business sponsor — the person who owns the budget?
This person's priorities override everything else in the project
Who are the end users of the reports and dashboards?
Their workflows determine the Gold layer and Power BI design
Who signs off on requirements and UAT?
No surprises at go-live — know the approver from day one
Is there a steering committee or weekly status meeting?
Understand where you need to report and at what cadence
Greenfield vs Migration — Assessment
Ask these questions to quickly establish which scenario you are in. Most engagements are not purely one or the other.
Scenario assessment
Is there an existing data platform — SSIS, ADF, Databricks, Synapse, Power BI?
If yes, migration. If no, greenfield
If existing — what is working well that must be preserved?
Never migrate away from something that works without a good reason
If existing — what is broken or painful that motivated this project?
The pain point is the real project goal
Is there a parallel run requirement — old and new running simultaneously?
Parallel runs double the work — understand this expectation early
Is the migration big bang or phased by source system?
Big bang is high risk — phased is safer but takes longer
What is the rollback plan if the new platform fails?
If there is no rollback plan, there is no go-live approval
Data Landscape Questions
Sources and volumes
What are all the source systems in scope?
Drives gateway vs cloud connection decisions
What is the approximate data volume — GB, TB?
Determines capacity SKU and partitioning strategy
What is the required refresh frequency — real-time, hourly, daily?
Real-time requires Eventstream, not batch pipelines
Are there any data quality issues in the source systems?
Silver layer complexity is proportional to source data quality
Who owns each source system — internal team or external vendor?
External vendors may have API limits or access restrictions
Are there any PII or sensitive data fields in scope?
Determines row-level security, masking, and Purview requirements
Phase 1 Exit — You Are Ready for Phase 2 When
  • ✅You know the deadline and whether it is fixed
  • ✅You have met every team member and know their skill level
  • ✅You know who the business sponsor and UAT approver are
  • ✅You have determined greenfield vs migration (or mix)
  • ✅You know all source systems and rough data volumes
  • ✅You have identified any compliance or PII requirements
  • ✅You have identified who will own the platform after you leave
💡 Discovery rarely takes one meeting. Budget at least one week of conversations before moving to Phase 2. Rushing discovery is the single biggest cause of rework later.
With discovery complete you now make the key architectural decisions and get everyone pointing in the same direction before a single line of code is written. This phase ends with a signed-off architecture diagram and agreed team standards.
Key Architecture Decisions to Make
1. Greenfield or Migration? Greenfield → start fresh, design for the future Migration → understand what must be preserved, plan phased cutover 2. Which Fabric workloads are in scope? Data Engineering → Lakehouse, Spark, Notebooks Data Warehouse → SQL, dimensional model Data Factory → Pipelines, Dataflows Power BI → Semantic models, reports Real-Time → Eventstream, KQL — only if near real-time needed 3. Medallion architecture layers needed? All three (Bronze/Silver/Gold) → standard, recommended Bronze + Gold only → acceptable for small/simple projects 4. Gold layer — Lakehouse or Data Warehouse? Lakehouse → Delta tables, flexible schema, large volumes Warehouse → T-SQL, dimensional model, Power BI DirectQuery 5. Power BI connectivity mode? Direct Lake → fastest, reads Delta directly, needs F64+ for full features DirectQuery → real-time but slower, any SKU Import → fastest for reports, stale by refresh schedule 6. On-premises sources present? Yes → gateway required → involve Infra team now No → cloud connections only → Fabric Admin handles alone
Scenario-Specific Approach
🌱 Greenfield
  • Design the medallion architecture from scratch
  • Define source system connections
  • Design Bronze ingestion pattern per source
  • Define Silver conformed layer schema
  • Define Gold star schema for reporting
  • Plan capacity sizing based on expected volume
  • No legacy constraints — design it right
🔄 Migration
  • Audit the existing platform thoroughly first
  • Map existing jobs/packages to Fabric equivalents
  • Identify what can be automated vs manual rebuild
  • Plan phased migration by source system
  • Define parallel run period and validation approach
  • Plan the cutover and rollback strategy
  • Carry forward what works, fix what does not

Team Alignment — Present the Standards
Call a team kickoff meeting. Present the standards guide. Get verbal agreement before anyone writes code.
  • 1Present fabric_standards_guide.html to the full data team — walk through each section
  • 2Get explicit agreement on naming conventions — no silent objections allowed
  • 3Get explicit agreement on workspace structure — DEV/TEST/PROD layout
  • 4Get explicit agreement on medallion layers — no skipping Bronze
  • 5Note any client-specific adjustments — update the standards doc before the meeting ends
  • 6Present the architecture diagram — show data flow from source to report
  • 7Get the business sponsor to sign off on the architecture approach
⚠ A standard that is not agreed in a meeting does not exist. If someone disagrees with a standard later and it was never discussed, the debate starts from scratch. Document every agreement and who was in the room.
Phase 2 Exit — You Are Ready for Phase 3 When
  • ✅Architecture diagram drawn and shared with team
  • ✅Greenfield vs migration approach decided and documented
  • ✅Which Fabric workloads are in scope — agreed
  • ✅Medallion layer design agreed
  • ✅Team standards presented and agreed in a meeting
  • ✅Business sponsor sign-off on the architecture approach
  • ✅For migration — legacy platform audit complete and mapping done
Set up the environment correctly so the team can build without blockers. This phase runs in parallel with Phase 2 — you are setting up the environment while aligning the team on standards. Do not wait for Phase 2 to be fully complete to start Phase 3.
Establish Checklist — In Order
  • 1 Hand fabric_admin_infra_guide.html to the relevant teams — Entra Admin, Infra, M365 Admin. Chase them daily until done.
  • 2 Confirm your consulting email has Fabric Admin role assigned — test by opening the Fabric Admin portal
  • 3 Create the three workspaces following the agreed naming convention — DEV, TEST, PROD
  • 4 Assign capacity to each workspace — DEV on smaller SKU, PROD on right-sized SKU
  • 5 Add team members to workspaces with correct roles — Admin, Member, Contributor, Viewer
  • 6 Create cloud connections for each source system in Manage Connections and Gateways
  • 7 Register Key Vault reference if pipelines need credentials — see fabric_admin_infra_guide.html Step 5
  • 8 Once gateway is installed by infra team — verify it appears as Online and configure on-prem source connections
  • 9 Create the control database and dbo.pipeline_control table for pipeline logging
  • 10 Connect DEV workspace to Git (Azure DevOps or GitHub) — branch: dev
  • 11 Create a hello world notebook in DEV — confirm Spark session starts, lakehouse attaches, Key Vault reads work
  • 12 Walk the team through the environment — every engineer should be able to log in, find their workspace, and open a notebook
💡 The hello world notebook is not optional. It proves the full stack works — workspace, capacity, lakehouse, Key Vault, and Spark — before the team starts building real pipelines. Finding a broken connection on hello world is far cheaper than finding it mid-sprint.

The longest phase. You are building the data platform while leading a team of engineers. Your role shifts — you are not doing all the coding yourself. You are setting direction, reviewing work, unblocking people, and ensuring standards are followed.
Your Role as Lead Architect in the Build Phase
You own → Architecture decisions — when the team hits a fork in the road, you decide → Code review — all PRs reviewed before merge to dev branch → Standards enforcement — you are the referee for the standards guide → Unblocking — when an engineer is stuck more than 2 hours, you step in → Stakeholder communication — weekly status, scope changes, risk escalation → Deployment to TEST and PROD — only you promote across environments Team owns → Feature branch development — each engineer owns their source or layer → Unit testing their own notebooks and pipelines → Raising PRs for review — not self-merging → Updating pipeline_control logs in every notebook they write → Flagging blockers to you immediately — not sitting on them
Build Order — Always in This Sequence
Bronze first — get raw data landing before anything else → One notebook per source entity → Watermark pattern from Day 1 → Control table logging from Day 1 → No transformations in Bronze → Prove the API / source connection works end-to-end Silver second — once Bronze is stable and reviewed → Type casting, null handling, deduplication → Upsert by natural key → Schema enforcement → Do not start Silver until Bronze is signed off Gold last — once Silver is stable → Star schema — fact and dimension tables → Aggregations and KPIs → Optimised for Power BI → Do not start Gold until Silver is signed off Power BI parallel — can start against Gold once first Gold table exists → Semantic model on top of Gold Delta tables → Direct Lake mode where possible → Reports built by BI developer, not data engineer

Code Review — What You Check on Every PR
  • 🔍Naming follows the standards guide — snake_case, correct prefix
  • 🔍No hardcoded secrets anywhere — all from Key Vault
  • 🔍Every function has a docstring and type hints
  • 🔍Try/except wraps every data operation — and re-raises after logging
  • 🔍Control table is updated — start and end of every run
  • 🔍Watermark is read before run and written after successful run
  • 🔍Bronze files land in the correct path structure
  • 🔍SQL uses parameterised queries — no f-strings for values
  • 🔍Comments explain WHY, not WHAT
⚠ A PR with a hardcoded secret is an instant rejection — rotate the secret immediately, do not just ask them to remove it from the code. Git history retains it even after deletion.
Greenfield vs Migration — Build Phase Differences
🌱 Greenfield Build
  • Build Bronze ingestion per source from scratch
  • Define schemas as you discover the data
  • No legacy code to reference
  • Freedom to design the right way
  • Risk: requirements may be vague early
  • Mitigation: demo early and often
🔄 Migration Build
  • Migrate one source system at a time
  • Run old and new in parallel per source
  • Validate row counts and totals match
  • Legacy code is reference — not copy-paste
  • Risk: hidden business logic in old code
  • Mitigation: involve the people who built the old system

  • ✅All Bronze notebooks running successfully in DEV with control table logging
  • ✅All Silver tables populated and validated against Bronze
  • ✅Gold layer tables built and semantic model connected

Before anything reaches PROD, it must be validated thoroughly. Validation is not just technical testing — it includes business sign-off, performance testing, and a security review. All three must pass.
Three Types of Validation Required
Technical validation — the data engineer's job → Does the pipeline run without errors? → Is the row count correct? → Are the data types correct? → Does the watermark advance correctly after each run? → Does the control table log success and failure correctly? Business validation (UAT) — the business stakeholder's job → Does the data match what they expect? → Do the report numbers match the source system? → Are the definitions correct? (e.g. what counts as an "active customer") → Can they find the information they need in the report? Performance and security — your job as architect → Does the pipeline complete within the required time window? → Are reports loading within acceptable response time? → Is RLS applied correctly — users only see what they should? → Is capacity usage within the purchased SKU limits?
Promoting DEV → TEST
TEST is where business users do UAT. It must look and behave like PROD.
  • 1Run the DEV → TEST promotion checklist from fabric_standards_guide.html
  • 2Use the Fabric deployment pipeline to promote items — not manual copy
  • 3Verify all connections point to TEST data sources — not DEV sources
  • 4Run all pipelines once in TEST — confirm control table shows success
  • 5Invite business users to TEST — provide UAT sign-off form

  • ✅Written UAT sign-off received from named business approver
  • ✅All UAT defects fixed and retested

Go-live is not the end — it is the beginning of handover. This phase ensures the client can run the platform without you. Never leave a client dependent on your phone number.
Go-Live Plan
Before go-live (at least 1 week before) → Promote TEST → PROD via deployment pipeline → Verify all connections point to PROD data sources → Run all pipelines once manually in PROD — verify success → Verify scheduled runs are configured and enabled → Confirm capacity is right-sized for PROD load — not paused → Confirm svc-fabricadmin owns all connections and Key Vault refs → Share rollback plan with client stakeholder Go-live day → Cut over on a Tuesday, Wednesday, or Thursday morning → Never go live on a Friday → Monitor the first scheduled pipeline run — stay online → Check control table shows success for all entities → Confirm business users can access reports Day after go-live → Review previous day's pipeline runs → Check capacity metrics — any throttling? → Address any issues raised by business users → Confirm scheduled runs completed overnight
Handover to Permanent Team
The permanent employee who will own this platform should have been shadowing you since Phase 3. By now they should be capable of running it independently. Handover is confirmation, not a crash course.
  • 1Walk the permanent admin through all four HTML guides — admin infra, architect, standards, and this runbook
  • 2Demonstrate how to check the control table for pipeline status — this is their daily health check
  • 3Show them the Fabric Capacity Metrics app — where to look if reports are slow
  • 4Walk them through the deployment pipeline — how to promote a fix from DEV to PROD
  • 5Confirm they can access all workspaces, connections, and Key Vault with their own credentials
  • 6Transfer all Fabric Admin responsibilities — they should be Fabric Admin before you leave, not after
Documentation Handover Checklist
  • 📄Architecture diagram — current state, not the original design
  • 📄fabric_admin_infra_guide.html — updated with client-specific details
  • 📄fabric_standards_guide.html — updated with any agreed client changes
  • 📄Source system inventory — what is connected, how, and who owns each source
  • 📄Key Vault secret names — what each secret is used for
  • 📄Scheduled pipeline schedule — what runs when and what it does
  • 📄Known limitations — anything that is a workaround or has a known issue
  • 📄Future roadmap items — features that were descoped or not yet built

  • ✅Permanent employee is Fabric Admin and can perform all admin tasks independently
  • ✅svc-fabricadmin owns all connections — not your email
  • ✅svc-fabricgateway owns the gateway registration — not your email
  • ✅All Key Vault references authorised by service account — not you

Some engagements include a hypercare period after go-live where you remain available for support. This phase is optional but common in large engagements. Define the hypercare terms clearly before it starts.
Define Hypercare Terms Before Go-Live
Duration : Typically 2–4 weeks after go-live Availability : Agree upfront — business hours only vs on-call Scope : Bug fixes only vs new features vs both Response time : P1 issues within X hours — agree the SLA Communication : Teams channel, email, or ticketing system End condition : What marks the end of hypercare — date or stability criteria
⚠ Without a defined end condition, hypercare becomes indefinite support. Agree a clear end date or stability criterion before go-live — "two weeks with no P1 incidents" is a good criterion.

Final Knowledge Transfer
  • 1Record a walkthrough video of the key platform components — permanent teams refer to this after you leave
  • 2Run a troubleshooting session with the permanent team — walk through common failure scenarios and how to diagnose them
  • 3Document the top 5 things that could go wrong and exactly what to do for each one
  • 4Confirm the permanent team knows how to rotate the Strava refresh token and update it in Key Vault
  • 5Leave a future roadmap note — what the next logical things to build are, so the team is not starting from a blank page after you leave
Phase 7 Exit — Engagement Complete When
  • ✅Hypercare period completed with no outstanding P1 issues
  • ✅Permanent team is running the platform independently without your help
  • ✅Final documentation handed over including troubleshooting guide
  • ✅Your consulting email fully removed from all systems
  • ✅Client sign-off on project completion received in writing
  • ✅Lessons learned documented for your own next engagement
